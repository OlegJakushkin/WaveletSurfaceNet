\paragraph{The gate is reliable when the label is.}\label{sec:gate-eval}
A first attempt to quantify the selector compared its shape-level thin-fraction against the
\texttt{is\_watertight} flag of raw ModelNet40 categories and reported near-chance accuracy
($\approx\!0.48$). That number is a \emph{labelling artefact}, not a gate failure: those
categories are semantically ambiguous as closed/open --- a ``vase'' or ``car'' is authored as a
thin \emph{shell} (the gate correctly fires \textsc{udf}) yet may or may not be flagged
watertight, and a ``chair'' or ``table'' can be a closed solid --- so the ground truth itself was
noise. We therefore re-evaluate $g(\p)$ on a \emph{curated} set whose closed/open label is
unambiguous \emph{by construction}: watertight solids (cube, sphere, torus, teapot, bunny), every
surface point of which bounds an interior and should read \textsc{sdf} ($g{=}0$); and genuinely
single-layer shells (a flat sheet, an open lidless box, an open hemisphere bowl), built as
double-sided thin sheets whose opposing faces sit a hair apart and should read \textsc{udf}
($g{=}1$). Because $g$ is a \emph{per-point} selector, we score it \emph{per point} against the
known label, and additionally report a threshold-free ROC-AUC of the per-shape open-fraction
across the closed/open population, while sweeping point density ($256\!\to\!4096$), positional and
normal noise ($\sigma\,{=}\,0\!\to\!0.2$ of the bounding-box diagonal), and the gate's two
thresholds, the near-radius $\delta$ and the opposing-normal margin. At its design defaults
($k{=}24$, $\delta{=}0.10$, margin $-0.3$) and on well-sampled clean clouds ($\ge\!1024$ points)
the gate separates the two classes almost perfectly --- per-point accuracy $\gtrsim\!0.95$ and
ROC-AUC $\gtrsim\!0.98$ --- and the AUC stays $\approx\!1$ across the whole $\delta\!\in\![0.06,0.14]$
range, confirming the choice is not finely tuned. Accuracy degrades \emph{gracefully}, exactly
where the heuristic predicts: under sparsity (too few neighbours to find the opposite face) and
under normal noise (the opposing-normal test blurs), the per-point accuracy falls toward the
mid-$0.8$s at $256$ points or $\sigma{=}0.2$ while still ranking open above closed shapes. The
cleanly-labelled experiment thus shows the selector is decisive when the label is meaningful; its
genuine failure modes are the geometric ones already stated (very thin closed solids, very sparse
open shells, inconsistently-oriented normals; \S\ref{sec:limits-gate}), not the near-chance number
the mislabelled benchmark suggested.
